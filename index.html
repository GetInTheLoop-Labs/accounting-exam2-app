Chapter 5: Cost-Volume-Profit (CVP) Analysis

CVP analysis examines the relationships between costs, volume, and profit. It helps managers understand how changes in costs and volume affect operating income — one of the most important tools in managerial accounting.

Core Formulas
CM = Sales - Variable Costs
CMu = Price per unit - Variable Cost per unit
CM% = CM / Sales (or CMu / Price)

Break-Even (units) = Fixed Costs / CMu
Break-Even ($) = Fixed Costs / CM%

Target Profit (units) = (FC + Target Profit) / CMu
Target Profit ($) = (FC + Target Profit) / CM%

MOS = Actual Sales - Break-Even Sales
MOS% = MOS / Sales

Operating Leverage = CM / Operating Income
OL x % Change in Sales = % Change in Op. Income

Contribution Margin Income Statement

Sales Revenue              $XXX
Less: Variable Costs        (XXX)
= Contribution Margin       XXX
Less: Fixed Costs           (XXX)
= Operating Income          $XXX
Appendix 5a: Cost Estimation
High-Low Method:
Variable Rate = (Cost_high - Cost_low) / (Activity_high - Activity_low)
Fixed Cost = Total Cost - (Variable Rate x Activity Level)
IMPORTANT: Use highest/lowest ACTIVITY levels, not cost levels!

Least-Squares Regression: Y = a + bX
a = fixed cost, b = variable cost/unit
R² closer to 1.0 = better fit

Key Concepts

Contribution Margin

Amount remaining from sales after variable expenses. Available to cover fixed costs, then provide profit. CM = Sales - Variable Costs.

CM Ratio (CM%)

Percentage of each sales dollar available for fixed costs and profit. CM% = CM / Sales. Example: CM% of 40% means $0.40 of every dollar covers fixed costs.

Break-Even Point

Where total revenue = total costs (operating income = $0). In units: FC / CMu. In dollars: FC / CM%. Sales above this point generate profit.

Target Profit

Sales needed to achieve a desired profit. Just add target profit to fixed costs in the break-even formulas: (FC + Target) / CMu or CM%.

Margin of Safety

How far sales can drop before losses begin. MOS = Actual Sales - BE Sales. MOS% = MOS / Sales. Higher MOS% = less risk.

Operating Leverage (OL)

Measures sensitivity of income to sales changes. OL = CM / Operating Income. OL x % change in sales = % change in income. High OL = high risk but high reward.

High-Low Method

Separates mixed costs using highest and lowest ACTIVITY points (not cost!). Calculate variable rate, then plug back in to find fixed cost.

Regression Analysis

Statistical method using ALL data points. Produces Y = a + bX. R² measures fit (closer to 1.0 = better). Most accurate of the three methods.

Chapter 6: Variable Costing & Segment Reporting

This chapter compares two methods of calculating product costs — variable costing and absorption costing — and introduces segmented income statements for evaluating business segments.

The Key Difference: How is Fixed MOH Treated?

Cost ComponentVariable CostingAbsorption Costing
Direct MaterialsProduct CostProduct Cost
Direct LaborProduct CostProduct Cost
Variable MOHProduct CostProduct Cost
Fixed MOHPeriod ExpenseProduct Cost
Unit Cost Formulas
Variable Costing: Unit Cost = DM + DL + Variable MOH
Absorption Costing: Unit Cost = DM + DL + Var MOH + (Fixed MOH / Units Produced)

When Do Incomes Differ?
Production = Sales: Same income under both methods
Production > Sales: Absorption income is HIGHER (fixed MOH deferred in inventory)
Production < Sales: Variable income is HIGHER (deferred MOH released)

Reconciliation: Absorption - Variable = Fixed MOH/unit x (Produced - Sold)

Segment Analysis
Segment Margin = CM - Traceable Fixed Costs
Segment BE ($) = Traceable Fixed Costs / CM%

Two Income Statement Formats

Variable Costing (CM Format)
Sales
- Variable COGS
- Variable S&A
= Contribution Margin
- Fixed MOH
- Fixed S&A
= Net Operating Income
Absorption Costing (Traditional)
Sales
- COGS (full cost)
= Gross Profit
- Selling Expenses
- Admin Expenses
= Net Operating Income

Key Concepts

Variable Costing

Only variable manufacturing costs are product costs (DM + DL + Variable MOH). Fixed MOH is a period expense — expensed entirely when incurred.

Absorption Costing

ALL manufacturing costs are product costs (DM + DL + Var MOH + Fixed MOH). Fixed MOH is included in inventory and expensed when units are SOLD.

Production > Sales Rule

When inventory increases: Absorption income > Variable income. Fixed MOH is "hidden" in inventory under absorption, not expensed yet.

Production < Sales Rule

When inventory decreases: Variable income > Absorption income. Previously deferred fixed MOH is released from inventory and expensed.

Contribution Margin vs Gross Profit

CM = Sales - ALL variable costs. Gross Profit = Sales - COGS (including fixed MOH). They are NOT the same!

Traceable Fixed Costs

Fixed costs directly attributable to a segment that would DISAPPEAR if the segment were eliminated. Examples: division manager salary, product-specific advertising.

Common Fixed Costs

Shared fixed costs that would NOT disappear if any single segment were eliminated. Examples: CEO salary, HQ rent. Do NOT allocate to segments for decisions.

Segment Margin

CM - Traceable Fixed Costs. If positive, the segment covers its own costs and contributes to common costs. Dropping it would hurt the company.

Chapter 8: Master Budgeting

A master budget is a comprehensive financial plan for the entire organization. The budgets must be prepared in a specific SEQUENCE because each builds on data from the previous one.

Master Budget Sequence (Order Matters!)

Sales Budget
→
Production Budget
→
Materials Budget
Direct Labor
→
MOH Budget
→
S&A Budget
Cash Budget
→
Budgeted I/S
→
Budgeted B/S
Operating Budget Formulas
Sales: Units Sold x Price = Revenue

Production: Budgeted Sales + Desired Ending FG - Beginning FG = Units to Produce

Merchandiser Purchases: Budgeted COGS + Desired Ending Inv - Beginning Inv = Purchases

Raw Materials: Production Needs + Desired Ending RM - Beginning RM = Materials to Purchase
  (Production Needs = Units to Produce x Material per Unit)

Direct Labor: Units to Produce x Hours/Unit x Rate/Hour = Total DL Cost

MOH: (Variable MOH Rate x Activity) + Fixed MOH = Total MOH
  Exclude depreciation from cash budget!

S&A: Variable S&A + Fixed S&A = Total S&A Expenses
Cash Budget Formulas
Cash Collections: Current period % collected + Prior period receivables
Cash Disbursements: Current period % paid + Prior period payables

Beginning Cash Balance
+ Cash Collections
- Cash Disbursements (materials, labor, OH less depreciation, S&A, equipment)
= Ending Balance (before financing)
+/- Borrowing or Repayment
= Ending Cash Balance

Ending A/R = Uncollected sales (e.g., 40% of month's sales if 60% collected same month)
Ending A/P = Unpaid purchases (e.g., 30% if 70% paid same month)

Key Concepts

Sales Budget

Starting point for the entire master budget. Forecasts unit sales and revenue by period. Everything else depends on this.

Production Budget

How many units to PRODUCE: Sales + Desired Ending FG - Beginning FG. The only budget stated in units only (not dollars).

Raw Materials Budget

How much material to BUY: Production needs + Desired Ending RM - Beginning RM. Convert to dollars by multiplying by cost per unit.

Direct Labor Budget

Units to Produce x Hours per Unit x Rate per Hour. Directly tied to production volume.

MOH Budget

Variable MOH (changes with production) + Fixed MOH (stays constant). Remember: depreciation is non-cash — include in budget but exclude from cash payments!

Cash Collections

Tracks WHEN cash is actually received. Must account for credit terms. Example: 60% same month + 40% next month. Don't forget beginning A/R balance.

Cash Budget

All cash in vs cash out. If ending balance < minimum required, must borrow. If excess > outstanding loans, can repay. Key exam topic!

Depreciation in Budgets

Include in MOH budget for income statement purposes. EXCLUDE from cash budget (it's non-cash). Factory depreciation = product cost (MOH). Delivery depreciation = operating expense.
